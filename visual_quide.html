Section 01 — The Core Problem

Why Do We
Need Super-Resolution?

Two satellites photograph the exact same piece of Earth. One sees it at 30 meters per pixel. The other sees it at 10 meters per pixel. The goal: use AI to make the blurry one as sharp as the clear one.

🛰 Landsat — Low Resolution
Each pixel covers a 30m × 30m area. That's the size of 3 basketball courts. Fine details like individual buildings, narrow roads, or small crop fields are invisible.
Simulated 30m resolution — blurry, blocky
🛰 Sentinel-2 — High Resolution
Each pixel covers a 10m × 10m area. Three times sharper than Landsat. You can see roads, field boundaries, and building rooftops clearly.
Simulated 10m resolution — sharp, detailed

🗓 Landsat Heritage
1972

Landsat has been photographing Earth since 1972 — over 50 years of historical data. This archive is scientifically irreplaceable but stuck at 30m resolution.

📐 Resolution Gap
3×

Sentinel-2 is 3× sharper than Landsat. The paper's goal is exactly this: apply 3× super-resolution magnification to make Landsat match Sentinel quality.

⚡ The Color Problem
≠

Even photographing the same land, Landsat and Sentinel produce different colors. This spectral mismatch is what breaks naïve super-resolution approaches.

Section 03 — Color Correction

Histogram Matching
Step by Step

Histogram Matching redistributes pixel brightness values so the source image's distribution matches the reference image. It's like matching the "brightness personality" of one image to another.

How It Works — 5 Steps
1
Count Pixel Brightnesses (Histogram)

For every brightness level (0=black to 255=white), count how many pixels have that value. Like taking attendance in a class — how many students sit at each "brightness desk"?

2
Calculate PDF (Probability Density Function)

Divide each count by total pixels → converts raw counts into percentages. "24% of pixels are at brightness level 180." Makes images comparable regardless of size.

3
Calculate CDF (Cumulative Distribution Function)

Running total of PDFs. CDF of 0.40 at value 100 means: "40% of all pixels are darker than 100." This is a ranking system — what percentile is each pixel?

4
Match Rankings Across Images

Key insight: a pixel at the 40th percentile in Source should become whatever value is at the 40th percentile in Reference. Same rank = same relative position = visually equivalent.

5
Remap All Pixels

Apply the mapping table to every pixel in the source image. Dark pixels get pushed up to brighter values. The resulting image has the same "brightness personality" as the reference.

Section 04 — Color Correction (Advanced)

Feature Distribution
Matching (FDM)

A smarter color correction that understands how colors relate to each other, not just how bright they are. Like matching the entire "mood" of the color palette.

HM vs FDM — What's the Difference?
HM
Histogram Matching

Adjusts each color channel independently. Matches brightness distribution channel by channel. Fast and mechanical — great for global brightness correction.

FDM
Feature Distribution Matching

Matches the mean AND covariance across all channels simultaneously. Understands how Red relates to Green, how Green relates to Blue. Captures the full "color personality."

The Math — Simply Put
FDM: Transfer μ (mean) + Σ (covariance)
from Reference → to Source

Mean (μ) = average color tone. Is the image generally warm or cool? Bright or dark?

Covariance (Σ) = how colors relate. When red is bright, is green also bright? FDM transfers both so the color relationships are preserved from reference to source.

Analogy: HM paints all walls the same shade as the reference room. FDM understands the entire mood and color scheme — warm tones, contrast relationships — and recreates that mood.

Section 05 — Detail Refinement

SR3 Diffusion Model

SR3 learns to sharpen images by first learning how to destroy them — then reversing that process. It adds real photographic detail that simple sharpening filters cannot.

Training: The Destruction Process
→
Start with sharp HR image

Take a perfectly sharp satellite image from the training set.

→
Gradually add noise (2000 steps)

At each step, add a tiny amount of random static. After 2000 steps: pure noise, original invisible.

→
SR3 learns to reverse each step

"Given image at step N, predict the noise that was added." After millions of examples, it becomes expert at denoising.

Inference: Reversing the Process
←
Start from noise + blurry guide

Begin with random static, plus the HM-corrected SwinIR output as a spatial guide.

←
Remove noise step by step

Each of 2000 steps removes a tiny bit of noise, guided by the blurry reference image.

←
Sharp image emerges

Like a sculptor revealing a statue from marble — 2000 careful chisel taps produce photorealistic detail.

Section 06 — Evaluation

How Do We Measure
Image Quality?

Three completely different ways to measure whether a super-resolved image is actually good. Each captures something different — and none of them is complete on its own.

PSNR
PEAK SIGNAL-TO-NOISE RATIO
PSNR = 10 × log₁₀( MAX² / MSE ) MSE = average squared pixel error ↑ Higher = Better

Measures raw pixel accuracy. For every pixel, computes (predicted - actual)², averages them all, then converts to decibels. Like measuring how close a dart thrower consistently hits the bullseye — in millimeters, converted to a score.

SSIM
STRUCTURAL SIMILARITY INDEX
SSIM = f( luminance × contrast × structure ) Range: 0 to 1 ↑ Higher = Better

Measures structural similarity by comparing local patches of 11×11 pixels. It asks: "Does this region look like the reference — in terms of edges, textures, and patterns?" PSNR is blind to blur; SSIM immediately detects it. You can blur an image, keep PSNR decent, but SSIM collapses.

LPIPS
LEARNED PERCEPTUAL IMAGE PATCH SIMILARITY
LPIPS = distance between VGG/AlexNet features Extracted by a pre-trained neural network ↓ Lower = Better

Uses a pre-trained image recognition network (VGG) to extract "what it sees" — not raw pixels but deep features. Measures how different these features are between two images. This is the closest approximation to "does this look right to a human?" Two images can have similar pixels but if one has weird halos or artifacts, LPIPS detects it.

Why Do We Need All Three?
PSNR

Pixel accuracy. Sensitive to tiny brightness errors even when the image looks perfect. Can be fooled by blur.

SSIM

Structural quality. Detects blur, edge degradation. Correlates well with human perception of sharpness.

LPIPS

Perceptual quality. Detects artifacts and texture errors invisible to PSNR/SSIM. Closest to human judgment.

Paper insight: FDM wins on PSNR and LPIPS. HM wins on SSIM. Neither method dominates all three metrics — which is why the paper uses both and recommends choosing based on your downstream application.

Section 08 — Physics

What Do Satellites
Actually Measure?

Satellites don't see colors directly. They measure how much light bounces back from Earth. This measured value goes through several conversions before it becomes scientifically useful.

DN
Digital Number (Raw Signal)

Raw electrical signal from the satellite sensor. Just a number (0–14000). Has no physical meaning on its own. Depends on sensor gain, temperature, and calibration. Formula: Lλ = G × DN + B

↓
TOA
Top-of-Atmosphere Reflectance

Physically meaningful — but still includes atmospheric distortion. Like seeing a tree through foggy glass. Formula: ρTOA = (π × Lλ × d²) / (Esun × cos θs)

↓
SR
Surface Reflectance (The Goal)

Atmospheric correction removes all atmospheric effects. Now measuring the actual land surface, as if no atmosphere existed. Consistent across time, season, and sensor. The paper's custom dataset uses this. Formula: ρsurf = (ρTOA − ρpath) / (Ts × Tv)

Applications of Surface Reflectance
🌿
Vegetation Monitoring
NDVI, EVI indices for crop and forest health
💧
Flood & Water Mapping
NDWI, MNDWI indices for water detection
🏔
Soil & Mineral Mapping
Spectral signatures distinguish surface materials
🌡
Climate Studies
Surface albedo for energy balance modeling
Why It Matters for SR

Regular RGB images have values 0–255. Satellite surface reflectance has values roughly 0–1 (or -0.1 to 0.5 depending on sensor). Training super-resolution on surface reflectance values makes the model:

✓ Physically meaningful — optimizes for real geophysical values
✓ Generalizable — consistent across time, geography, sensors
✓ Directly usable in downstream science (no extra conversion)

What "Harmonized" Actually Means

The HLS pipeline does 4 things to make Landsat and Sentinel data truly comparable:

01
Atmospheric Correction

Removes the effect of atmosphere (dust, water vapor). Both satellites now see the same surface.

02
BRDF Normalization

Corrects for sun angle differences. Photos at different times of day now have consistent brightness.

03
Spatial Co-registration

Aligns both satellite images to the exact same coordinate grid. Pixels correspond to the same ground location.

04
Band Matching

Maps equivalent spectral bands between sensors. Landsat Red ≈ Sentinel Red after this correction.

Transfer Learning — Why Not Train From Scratch?

SwinIR was originally trained on natural photographs (cats, buildings, landscapes). The paper freezes the first 3 of 6 transformer blocks and only fine-tunes the last 3 on satellite data.

BLOCKS 1-3
🔒 Frozen
Edges, textures
BLOCKS 4-6
🔓 Fine-tuned
Satellite-specific
Analogy: Like hiring someone who already knows how to read — you don't reteach reading, you just train them for your specific domain. Natural images and satellite images both have edges and textures. The early layers already know this. Only the later layers need to adapt.

Benefits: Less training time, less data needed, prevents overfitting on small satellite datasets.
